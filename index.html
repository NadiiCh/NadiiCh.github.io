Ellipsis

This is a concise guide to ellipsis that presents the definition, classification, types and useful literature on ellipsis.

Definition Classification Types Useful Sources

Definition

The term ellipsis refers to a phenomenon when a part of syntactic material is missing in the sentence, as presented below:

  • He wanted to surprise Kate, but he didn't know how [to surprise her].
  • Jack didn't read the book, but I did [read the book].

Interestingly, although some material is elided, the meaning of both sentences is preserved. This is possible due to an antecedent. For instance, an antecedent for 'I did [read the book]' is the rightmost part of the sentence, namely 'Jack didn't read the book'.

Classification

We can group ellipsis according to how much syntactic material is elided:

  • Predicate (verbal) ellipsis - a type of ellipsis, in which a predicate is missing:

    She has never been to Rome but Ivan has [been to Rome].

    Kate watched this film, but Anne didn't [watch this film].

  • Clausal ellipsis - a type of ellipsis, in which an entire clause is missing:

    Jack bought something, but I don't know what [Jack bought].

    A: What did you eat?
    B: [I ate] A banana.

  • Nominal ellipsis - a type of ellipsis, in which a head noun is missing:

    Polish: Kasia lubi francuskie filmy, a Ania lubi tylko w≈Çoskie [filmy].

    Translation: 'Kate likes French movies, and Anne likes only Italian [movies].'

Info! Although there is no theoretical background that would support this classification, it can be found in various publications on ellipsis.

Types

This section presents the most common types of ellipsis.

Type Example
Sluicing John can speak two languages, but I don't know which [languages he can speak].
Verb Phrase Ellipsis (VPE) Mary likes coffee, but Jane doesn't [like coffee].
Modal Ellipsis (M(C)E) Lucy can't speak French, but Ian can [speak French].
Gapping Tom can play the guitar, and Sarah [can play] the piano.
Pseudogapping Frank will give flowers to Mary, but he won't [give flowers] to Sarah.
Stripping Henry lives in Warsaw and Simon [lives in Warsaw] too.
Fragment answers A: What did you see?
B: [I saw] A bird.
Null Complement Anaphora (NCA) I didn't take her to the cinema, even though I promised [to take her to the cinema].
